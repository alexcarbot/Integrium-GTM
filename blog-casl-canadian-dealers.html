CASL for Canadian Dealers: What Changes in 2026 and How to Prepare

IL
Integrium Legal 8 min read

Understanding CASL: The Foundation

Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation — commonly known as CASL — is one of the strictest electronic messaging laws in the world. Enacted in 2014, it governs the sending of commercial electronic messages (CEMs) to or from Canada, including emails, text messages, and certain social media communications. For Canadian automotive dealers, CASL is not a peripheral regulatory concern — it is a daily operational reality that touches every customer interaction conducted through digital channels.

CASL applies to any electronic message that encourages participation in a commercial activity. For dealers, this includes appointment confirmations, service reminders, promotional offers, follow-up messages after a lead submission, and even thank-you emails that reference products or services. The scope is broad by design, and the enforcement mechanism carries meaningful financial penalties.

Despite having been in effect for over a decade, CASL compliance remains inconsistent across the automotive retail sector. Many dealers operate with outdated consent records, incomplete unsubscribe mechanisms, and limited understanding of the distinction between express and implied consent. The 2026 enforcement updates make addressing these gaps urgent.

What Is Changing in 2026

The Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC) and the Competition Bureau have signaled a more aggressive enforcement posture for 2026, with several developments that directly affect dealership operations:

  • Expanded enforcement scope: The CRTC has indicated it will extend its enforcement focus beyond large-scale spammers to include mid-market businesses with systemic compliance gaps, including multi-location retail operations like auto dealerships.
  • Heightened consent documentation requirements: Regulators are placing greater emphasis on the ability to produce evidence of consent on demand. Having consent in practice is no longer sufficient — dealers must demonstrate that consent was obtained, when it was obtained, what was communicated at the time, and how it has been maintained.
  • AI and automated messaging scrutiny: As dealerships adopt AI-powered communication tools, regulators have signaled that automated messages are subject to the same consent requirements as human-sent messages. The use of AI does not create an exemption or a grey area.
  • Cross-border enforcement coordination: Increased cooperation between Canadian and international regulators means that dealers operating near the U.S. border or serving cross-border customers face compliance obligations under both CASL and applicable U.S. regulations like the TCPA.

"The 2026 guidelines make clear that CASL compliance is not a one-time checkbox — it is an ongoing obligation that requires documented processes, regular audits, and technology systems capable of enforcing consent rules at every touchpoint."

Express vs. Implied Consent: What Dealers Must Know

The distinction between express and implied consent is the single most important concept in CASL compliance, and it is where most dealerships encounter problems.

Express Consent

Express consent is an affirmative, opt-in agreement from the recipient to receive commercial electronic messages. It must be obtained through a clear, specific request that identifies the sender, describes the types of messages that will be sent, and includes contact information. Express consent does not expire — it remains valid until the recipient withdraws it.

For dealers, express consent is typically obtained through:

  • Website lead forms with a clearly labeled, unchecked consent checkbox
  • In-dealership forms signed during a vehicle purchase or service visit
  • Explicit opt-in during phone conversations, documented in the CRM
  • Digital signature on a consent-specific document at the point of sale

Pre-checked boxes, bundled consent (buried in terms and conditions), and assumed consent based on a business relationship do not qualify as express consent under CASL.

Implied Consent

Implied consent exists under specific, time-limited circumstances defined by the legislation. For dealers, the most relevant scenarios are:

  • Existing business relationship: If a customer has purchased or leased a vehicle, or received service, within the past two years, implied consent exists to send CEMs. This clock starts from the date of the most recent transaction.
  • Inquiry-based relationship: If a person submits an inquiry — a web lead, a phone call, a chat interaction — implied consent exists for six months from the date of the inquiry.
  • Conspicuously published address: If a business contact has published their email address publicly (e.g., on a company website) without a statement that they do not wish to receive unsolicited CEMs, implied consent may exist, but only for messages relevant to their role.

Critical timeline: Implied consent from an inquiry expires after six months. For a lead that submits a form in January, your consent window closes in July. If you have not converted that lead to a customer (establishing a new two-year business relationship) or obtained express consent by that point, you must stop sending CEMs. Many dealerships overlook this deadline, creating significant compliance exposure in their long-term nurture campaigns.

Electronic Message Requirements

Every commercial electronic message sent under CASL must meet three structural requirements, regardless of whether consent is express or implied:

  1. Sender identification: The message must clearly identify who is sending it. For dealerships, this means the legal business name (not just a DBA or brand name), a physical mailing address, and either a phone number, email address, or URL. This information must be accurate and current.
  2. Unsubscribe mechanism: Every CEM must include a functional unsubscribe mechanism that is easy to use, free of charge, and effective within 10 business days. The mechanism must remain active for at least 60 days after the message is sent. Link-based unsubscribe is the standard approach, but replying with "STOP" is also acceptable for SMS.
  3. Truthful subject lines and content: The message cannot use deceptive subject lines, false sender information, or misleading content. This requirement is straightforward but occasionally violated by aggressive promotional templates.

Record-Keeping Obligations

CASL compliance is only as strong as your documentation. In an enforcement action, the burden falls on the sender to prove that consent existed at the time the message was sent. This means dealers must maintain comprehensive records that include:

  • The date, time, and method of consent collection for every contact
  • The specific language presented at the time consent was obtained
  • The consent type (express or implied) and its basis (purchase, inquiry, opt-in form)
  • Expiration dates for implied consent records
  • A complete log of unsubscribe requests and the dates they were processed
  • Evidence that unsubscribe requests were honored within the required timeframe

For multi-rooftop dealer groups, these records must be maintained at the entity level. Consent given to one dealership in a group does not automatically extend to sister stores unless the consent language explicitly covered communications from affiliated entities.

Penalties and Enforcement

CASL penalties are among the highest in the world for anti-spam legislation. Individuals face fines of up to $1 million per violation, while corporations can be penalized up to $10 million per violation. These are maximum figures, and actual penalties are assessed based on factors including the nature of the violation, the organization's compliance history, and whether the violation was knowing or negligent.

Beyond direct fines, CASL violations carry reputational risk and operational disruption. An enforcement action requires legal response, diverts management attention, and can affect relationships with OEM partners who have their own compliance standards for authorized dealers.

The CRTC also has the authority to issue compliance notices and undertakings — essentially requiring organizations to implement specific corrective measures under regulatory supervision. For a dealership, this could mean externally monitored overhauls of communication processes, consent databases, and staff training programs.

Practical Compliance Checklist for Dealers

The following checklist provides a structured approach to achieving and maintaining CASL compliance. It is designed for immediate use and should be reviewed quarterly.

Consent Management

  • Audit all lead capture forms to confirm they include clear, unchecked consent checkboxes with compliant language
  • Review in-store forms and digital signature documents for CASL-specific consent provisions
  • Confirm that CRM records include consent type, date, and method for every active contact
  • Implement automated expiration tracking for implied consent (6-month inquiry, 2-year business relationship)
  • Establish a process to convert implied consent contacts to express consent before the implied window closes

Message Compliance

  • Verify that every outbound CEM template includes complete sender identification (legal name, address, contact info)
  • Test all unsubscribe mechanisms to confirm they function correctly and process within 10 business days
  • Review automated sequences (AI-generated messages, drip campaigns, service reminders) for CASL compliance
  • Confirm that SMS communications include a STOP opt-out keyword and that responses are processed immediately
  • Audit subject lines and content for accuracy — remove any language that could be considered deceptive

Operational Controls

  • Designate a CASL compliance officer or point of contact within the dealership
  • Conduct annual CASL training for all staff involved in customer communication, including BDC agents, sales, and service advisors
  • Implement technology controls that prevent sending CEMs to contacts without valid consent records
  • Establish a quarterly audit process to review consent records, unsubscribe logs, and message templates
  • Document all compliance activities, training sessions, and audit findings for potential regulatory review

"CASL compliance is not about restricting your ability to communicate with customers. It is about building a consent-based communication framework that protects both your customers and your business. Dealers who get this right build stronger relationships and avoid the significant financial and reputational risks of non-compliance."

The 2026 enforcement landscape makes CASL compliance a strategic priority, not just a legal obligation. Dealers who proactively audit their processes, invest in proper consent management technology, and train their teams will operate with confidence. Those who continue to treat CASL as a background concern are accepting a level of risk that is increasingly difficult to justify. The regulatory environment is tightening, the penalties are real, and the tools to comply effectively are available today.
